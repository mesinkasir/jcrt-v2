The City and the Stars: Politics and Alterity in Heidegger, Levinas and Blanchot
================================================================================

Lars Iyer\
University of Newcastle upon Tyne

* * * * *

> *The disaster: break with the star, break with every form of totality ...* (Blanchot, *Writing of the Disaster*, 75)

![I](https://web.archive.org/web/20050213083536im_/http://www.jcrt.org/images/alpha/I.gif)n Plato's *Laws*, the Athenian stranger claims that there are two arguments in particular that foster the belief in the gods -- the antiquity and divinity of the soul and the order of the heavens under the control of reason. No one who has contemplated the heavenly bodies carefully could suppose that celestial events happen by necessity rather than divine intention (*Laws* 966d). And indeed, the stranger maintains, no one can govern the *polis* who does not understand that the stars are steered by a divine will and exist in harmony with customs and law [*nomos*] (*Laws* 968). But what happens when this contemplation reveals neither the order of the soul nor the order of the heavens? What happens with the awareness that the disarray of the universe mirrors the disarray of the soul and it is no longer possible to harmonize the affairs of state according to a *nomos* that encompasses heaven and earth? The *polis* is cast adrift and our condition becomes, in John Caputo's word, *disastrous*, since the stars, *astres*, have fallen (*Against Ethics *6). One can discern a desire to address this fallenness in the search for a lost moral unity, with the cohesion and solidarity of a society founded on hard work and co-operation. One can also find it in the attempt to revive a threatened spiritual unity by seeking, for example, a new embodiment of the early Christian community or the Muslim caliphate. But these attempts to re-harmonize individual, familial and societal responsibilities occur amidst the reign of efficiency and optimization, where the hegemony of the marketplace threatens to overturn old models of being-together. We cannot simply restore the missing *nomos*, placing the stars back in the sky. Indeed, we are no longer sure whether we know who 'we' are -- consumers? citizens? subjects? As Caputo reflects, "It is not a question of knowing what to put in their place, but of just getting along without such a place, of conceding that things are just 'decentered', 'disseminated', 'disastered'. I would say that we are in a fix, except that even to say 'we' is to get into a still deeper fix. We are in the fix that we cannot say 'we'" (*Against Ethics *6). How then can we endure this "disastronomic, disastrological, deconstructive setback" without the model of a stellar *nomos*, a stellar *logos* that could serve as the image of the loftiness of the law? (*Against Ethics *6)

### I

> *We are too late for the gods and too / Early for Being, Being's poem / Just begun, is man. // To head toward a star -- this only. // To think is to confine yourself to a / single thought that one day stands / still like a star in the world's sky* ("The Thinker as Poet," 4)

    > In all the temples of the gods and in all sites of human habitation, this fire has its secure locale and, as this locale, gathers around it all that properly occurs [*sich ereignet*] and is bestowed. Through this fire, the hearth is the enduring ground and determinative middle -- the site of all sites, as it were, the homestead pure and simple, toward which everything presences alongside and together with everything else and thus first is (*Ister* 105).

    > Heidegger would have us attend to the upsurge of the unhomely in this burning, that is, the illumination and brightness that permits the homely to appear. This emerging and arising grants the opening of the *polis*; it is the origin that does not cease springing forth, surpasses the ordinary, familiar, "homely" conception of beings. The hearth is a kind of event, an *Ereignis*, a happening that properly occurs [*sich ereignet*], as the middle, gathering everything around it and bestowing a home, a site to all beings.

    > it could also be that the Germans -- granted that they learn to use freely what is their own and do not evade the conditions required for such learning -- might, in what is foreign to them [...] come to excel what is proper to the Greeks. If, that is, they have become more open, so that "what illuminates" (the heavens' is "open to our open view" [....] It could be that a "guest-house" and establishment might be founded and build for the gods, one that the Greek temples can no longer approach (*Ister* 124).

    If it is impossible simply to reproduce Greece, to build temples to welcome the return of the Greek gods, or to open new hearths in the homes, how might dwelling become possible for the Germans? How can the hearth restore the relation to the sky and the earth?

    > the dif-ference does not mediate after the fact by connecting world and things through a middle added on to them. Being the middle, it first determines world and things in their presence, i.e. in their being toward one another, whose unity carries out. (*On the Way to Language* 202)

    > In the sense in which Heidegger uses the word, *Dichtung* refers to a certain creative invention, a projective saying [*entwerfende Sagen*] that determines the forms of possible experience for a people, throwing it forward into the unhomely. *Dichtung* refers to something much more than poetry. To claim that language "is *Dichtung* in the essential sense" ("The Origin of the Work of Art" 61/199), is to link language to a saying [*die Sage*] or a showing [*die Zeige*] of what is possible for a people. It is even linked, Heidegger suggests, to saga and legends [*die Sagen*] (*On the Way to Language* 252/122). Language, as *Dichtung*, grants the co-belonging of gods, mortals, world and earth, the fourfold in which a people can make a habitation.

### II

> *Modern humanist man is a man in a State. Such a man is not merely vulgar; he is religion's true antagonist within the State of Israel itself [....] It is obvious that Israel asserts itself in a different way. Like an empire on which the sun never sets, a religious history extends the size of its modest territory, even to the point where it absorbs a breath-taking past. But, contrary to national histories, this past, like an ancient civilization, places itself above nations, like a fixed star. And yet we are the living ladder that reaches up to the sky.* ("Difficult Freedom" 260)

    > Heidegger, with the whole of Western history, takes the relation with the Other as enacted in the destiny of sedentary peoples, the possessors and builders of the earth. Possession is pre-eminently the form in which the other becomes the same, by becoming mine (*Totality and Infinity* 46).

    > For Heidegger, the Greeks build and thereby take possession of the earth, but the hearth remains cannot be so possessed. What is extraordinary about the Greeks for Heidegger is the way in which they maintain the unhomely at the center of all relations.[^2] In this sense, the Greeks belong to the *polis* because they do not seek to *determine* the hearth. Likewise, it is this that allowed them to experience the *polis* in a sense radically different to the politics of today as it answers to the canonical meditations of Plato and Aristotle. But what is questionable about Heidegger's exemplification of the Greeks, for Levinas, is the subordination he continues to maintain regarding the relation to the Other:

    > Heideggerian ontology, which subordinates the relationship with the Other to the relation with Being in general, remains under obedience to the anonymous, and leads inevitably to another power, to imperialist domination, to tyranny. (*Totality and Infinity* 47-48)

    Levinas does not dispute the fact that the *polis* provides a certain unity to the relations of the people who belong to it: it does indeed gather and bestow a unity upon the relation to the gods, to festivals, to sacrifice and battle, honor and glory, the relationship between master and slave. But it does not answer to the relation to the Other as *Autrui*; nor does it include the relation to the Levinasian God. The articulation of its unity is repressive; it constitutes an immanence or totality, closing itself off from the relation to which Levinas would attend. But might it be possible to answer this relation?

*  *  *

    > man is [...] the irruption of God within Being, or the bursting out of Being towards God; man is the fracture in Being which produces the act of giving, with hands which are full, in place of fighting and pillaging. This is where the idea of being chosen comes from, an idea which can deteriorate into pride, but originally expresses the awareness of an appointment which cannot be called into question; an appointment which is the basis of ethics and which, through its indisputability, isolates the person in his responsibility. ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 202)

    These dense sentences recapitulate the arguments of other texts, of the irruption into the order of being that receives, Levinas claims, its classic elaboration in Descartes' account of the idea of the infinite.[^3] Unlike Descartes, Levinas does not suppose the idea of the infinite to refer to a being so perfect that it must exist, but opens beyond the totality of being. It is to this he alludes a few pages later:

    > The worry is induced by man by God's Infinity, which he can never contain, but which inspires him -- inspiration being the original mode of worry, the inspiration of man by God constituting man's humanity; and the "within" of this "disproportionate within the finite" only becomes possible through the "here I am" of the man welcoming his neighbor. Listening to the Muse dictating one's songs is not the original form of inspiration; instead, it lies in obedience to the Most High by way of the ethical relationship with the Other. ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 207)

    Why does Heidegger heed the poetic Muse rather than the word of God?

[^1]: The literature on Heidegger's Nazism is massive. Relevant for the present essay is Bestigui's *Heidegger and the Political*, particularly as it explores the function of the readings of Sophocles's *Antigone* in *Introduction to Metaphysics* and *Hölderlin's Hymn "The Ister,"* reflecting on the relationship between the Greeks and Hölderlin.

[^2]:  My aim is to show how the overcoming of certain nostalgia for a lost authority, tradition or religion exhibited in different ways in Heidegger and Levinas might permit us to rethink our relation to others and to the world. Both thinkers offer a partial solution to the question as to how we might live in the midst of a growing secularism and dissatisfaction with existing models of politics. For both, as I will argue, it is a question of setting up a relationship between the *polis*, the locus of politics and a certain alterity, figured by the "mono-astronomical" image of a star (*Against Ethics* 229). By contrast, the disastrous community, which I will set out with reference to the writings of Blanchot, names a way of being together that is no longer fixed upon older models of the political. It is not, as I will show, a question of getting out of our "fix," but of understanding why it is impossible to fix this fix by dreaming of an unanimity to come. Blanchot, as I will argue, grants us the possibility of articulating a disastrous politics, a politics of the dis-aster, *dés-astre*, that would permit us to look out for new spaces of freedom that break open in our present and to stand guard against the foreclosure of the political space.

[^6]:  It is by attempting to think politics *from* the Greek experience of the *polis* that Heidegger would raise the question concerning the essence of the contemporary conception of politics. Upstream of the determination of the *polis* as the state [*Staat*] or the city [*Stadt*], Heidegger looks to the *stead* [*die Statt*] as "the site [*die Stätte*] of the abode of human history that belongs to humans in the midst of beings" (*Ister* 82). The essence of the *polis* must be thought from the *polos*, the pole, as the axis around which everything moves. The *polis* as *polos* is the site of the abode to which everything is related, that is, to *beings as a whole*.

[^15]: See Bataille, *Literature and Evil*. Bataille, indeed, is the third author alongside Levinas and Heidegger Blanchot claims in *The Writing of the Disaster* as a thinker of the gift (see 108-111). The enigmatic pages that the names of the three authors who most influenced Blanchot are brought together demand close attention. For Blanchot, Bataille and Levinas share a notion of the gift "as the inexhaustible (the infinite) demand of the other and of others, a demand that calls for nothing less than impossible loss: the gift of interiority"; Heidegger also allows the questions "Who gives? What is given?" to resound without determining an answer (110). In all three thinkers, "these are questions which no formulation suits, and which resound in language without receiving any answer than language itself, the gift of language" (110). In Levinas, the gift is thought both in terms of the relation to the Other and as the *il y a*; in Heidegger, in the alleged generosity of the *es gibt* linked to the truth of being and in Bataille, at least in *Literature and Evil*, to evil. For Blanchot these stagings of donation converge in a certain determination of language in all three thinkers. But is it not, precisely, *Blanchot* who carries through in the most rigorous and intractable way this mediation on language? Unlike Levinas, he opens up the question of language in terms of the *il y a*, of existence in general, both in his novels and in his theoretical writings. Unlike Heidegger, he retreats from positing a homeostatis between world and earth such that a historical community could come into being. What, then, is the relation between Bataille and Blanchot? I cannot examine this question here; see Iyer's "Born With the Dead." Is the gift a name for the witnessing? Is witnessing in all the thinkers under discussion, albeit in differing ways, the gift of interiority?

[^16]: For Heidegger, Hölderlin's poetry is disclosive in a sense analogous to the basic words of Greek thinkers. Hölderlin provides not only a diagnosis of the modern German need for a new disclosure, but indicates the way in which this happening, this *Ereignis*, could bring beings, gods, men and earth into relation as a world (see *"Germanien" und "Der Rhein"* 183). This is not to be accomplished through a simple *imitation* of classical Greek civilization. Hölderlin knows that the Greek gods are lost and cannot return. The gods belonged to the Greeks until the God of the Christians came, bestowing new worlds and new relations to the earth. But those relationships have declined in turn. It is Hölderlin who, for Heidegger, understands how this decline is linked to the crisis of a historical people.

[^16]: Here, one might recall a passage from *The Unavowable Community* where Blanchot remembers the "people of Paris," that is, those thousands of individuals who came together to remember those killed at the Charonne Metro Station as part of the movement against French colonialism in Algeria (33). "Inert, immobile, less a gathering than the always imminent dispersal of a presence momentarily occupying the whole space and nevertheless without a place (utopia), a kind of messianism announcing nothing but its autonomy and its *worklessness* (on the condition that it be left to itself, or else it will change immediately and become a network of forces ready to break loose): thus are mankind's people whom it is permissible to consider as the bastardized imitation of God's people (rather similar to what could have been the gathering of the children of Israel in view of the Exodus if they had gathered while at the same time forgetting to leave ..." (33). No, the call in question is not linked with any particular people -- but it can be linked nonetheless to a Jewish bearing witness of a certain kind.

[^17]: In the period of another crisis for Germany, Heidegger looks to the poet for a new dispensation of language, of a saying, a creative *Dichtung*, that would permit a new relation that would open the German people to the unhomely and hence a new home.[^1] In his authorship of this period, Heidegger would, like Heraclitus, warm his hands by a hearth where he tells us that the gods are present. But the hearth, now, is the *poem*. Heidegger looks to poetry for *Dichtung* because language itself has shrunk from its inventive possibilities. It cannot heed its own potential because it has become merely a medium for transmitting information. Only the poem keeps a memory of the farthest reaches of what is possible for a people to say. It points, even now, to another way of inhabiting the earth, of relating to the heavens and opening a world because of the originary openness to which it maintains an essential relation. But it does so only to those who can heed the poem, that is, *read* it.

[^17]: On Blanchot's notion of community, see his *The Unavowable Community*, part of which was written in response to an essay by Nancy later included in *The Inoperative Community* which itself responds to Blanchot's response. The notion of community, Blanchot insists, cannot be translated as *Volk*, which is to say, into the language of "The Origin of the Work of Art" and, finally, "The Self Assertion of the German University".

[^18]: Heidegger's withdrawal from political life is a turn from an institutionalization of politics, from the contemporary determination of the political. It is a retreat to reading, the hearth, an *active* movement to bring a new dispensation of the unhomely, for a new dispensation of presencing. It is to hope for another beginning, for a gathering that would ground and unify a people to come in a new being-together no longer ordered by the demands of contemporary politics. To invent a politics out of the inventive possibilities of *Dichtung* demands a new turbulence, a new swirling [*Wirbel*] of the *polis*, a new essence and a new *dynamis*. Hölderlin's poems point, for Heidegger, to the still star in the world's sky, promising, for those who would know how to read them, a new communication with the heavenly abode and hence with beings as a whole.

[^18]: See, in this connection, "Waiting", reprinted in *The Blanchot Reader*, originally published in a *festschrift* for Heidegger.

[^21]: "That is my place in the sun. That is how the usurpation of the whole world began": Pascal's epigram, for Levinas, belies a more originary appropriation (*Pensées* 141). What is it, then, that prevents Heidegger from attending to this relation?

[^24]: For Heidegger, being is finite; in-finity is merely privative. The understanding of being of a people cannot be added to the understanding of being of another in order to gain a better understanding of being as a whole. Being is never disclosed all at once. From the 1930s onwards, Heidegger sketches the history of being, the *Seinsgeschichte* of epochs that succeed one another. Each epoch is opened by an event, by an occurrence of the truth of being; but each is also subject to the possibility of the decline of that truth. The hearth itself can be extinguished; the relation between the *polis* and the founding event can wither. This lability, this *Verfallen*, is implicit in the finitude of being. For Heidegger, to attribute infinitude to being would suggest that being is always available, that there is no *Verfallen*, no retreat and no possibility of decline. This is why one might assume that politics is simply a matter of administration or economic programming.

[^25]: For Levinas, the human being is not the seeker or the preserver of truth of being; God has not died, the Revelation is an ongoing event. To think the infinite, for Levinas, is to receive the Revelation to which Judaism bears witness with an absolute exemplarity. On this account, the Heideggerian thought of finitude excludes the infinite by contracting, by folding itself into an enclosed space. Judaism, with its relation to the infinite, attests to this outside, as does Levinas's thought in general. It is not by chance that *Otherwise Than Being* is dedicated to the victims of anti-Semitism, or that this anti-Semitism is understood to refer to the hatred of the Other, the same hatred, indeed, of those assassinated by all confessions and all nations.

[^26]: For Levinas, Heidegger was not an innocent bystander in the crisis of Nazism. But to make this point is not to recommend the abandonment of Heideggerian philosophy. It is to locate Heidegger in the history of the representation of the Other that stems not merely from the Roman *imperium* but from the Greek *polis*. Levinas would show how a Judaic *topos* is folded into the Greek one: how another conception of the relation to the *polis*, to politics, has been concealed.

[^27]: The relation to the Other finds each of us, just as the voice of God moving through the garden found Adam. The relation to the Other permits no possible evasion. I have always and already responded that I am already committed to the relation to which I attest in my own voice. Rigorously distinguishing his notion from Heidegger's *Sagen*, Levinas calls this witnessing *le Dit*, the *to-say*, or *saying*. The order of discourse, the said, is inadequate to the saying that attests to the originary disappropriation of the "I," to the prior rupture of interiority, to the dissipation of every secret.

[^28]: Levinas's scriptural commentaries are an attempt to attend to the Revelation, to the saying that reverberates in the scriptures. His philosophical writings are likewise aimed at demonstrating the structure of this Revelation. Levinas demands new modes of elaboration of the Revelation, retrieving the witnessing, the saying of its texts over the letter of the system of true facts and triumphs of those disciplines, psychoanalysis, sociology and political economy that would proclaim the end of metaphysics (see "Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 193). It is a practice of exegesis that would awaken the saying of the scriptures -- the search for a meaning that would activate the Revelation for a contemporary audience through seeking and deciphering, through writing and rewriting: "The reader is, in his own fashion, a scribe" ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 194). Reading is a way of belonging to the Revelation, granting it new life in the interiority of the reader. This is why the scriptures are rewritten in the uniqueness of reading and it is the multiplicity of this people of readers that allows the voice of the Revelation, the truth of the whole, to resound (see "Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 195). The sacred books are a confluence of texts that, like the strings of a violin stretched over its wood, resound under the bow of the commentator. But the Jews are like the body of the violin that allow the song to resound. It is this tradition of exegesis, of reading, that is also a ceaseless rewriting that make Israel the "people of the Book" by virtue of its land, an extension of its manuscripts and scrolls ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 199).

[^29]: In both his confessional and his philosophical writings, Levinas asks us to attend to the "good" infinitude of the encounter with the human other that, in advance, displaces the "I" of its relation to itself. His confessional writings point to Abraham's experience of a call from without, of a relation that commands a nomadism and dispersal. The *polis* cannot contain the Jew; the "I" cannot contain the "I." The human being irrupts towards God and bursts out of the city. The Jew is appointed to bear witness to this relation, but all of us are isolated, upstream of the powers and the mastery of the "I," in a similar responsibility.

[^30]: In this sense, the Heideggerian *polis*, the whole of being, is exposed every time Levinasian saying occurs, which is to say in any word, any gesture, any text, any transmission. Of course, Heidegger also insists on the importance of *Sagen*, of reading poets, of attending to the Greeks more primordially than the Greeks. But Levinas demurs; the relation to the infinite is yet more immediate: it has already broken through *in* *philosophy*, as the originary interruption of a certain Greek tradition of which Heidegger's thought is the latest and most powerful incarnation, and *in* *the experience of the subject*, the interruption of its autonomy.

[^31]: Mine is not the place in the sun. Levinas bears witness to this originary witnessing, to an encounter that threatens its return, to the idea of the infinite as it awakens the self, of a thought that is "over-full" ["*trop-plein*"] such that it worries the "I," leaving it in a state of non-repose. In greeting the Other, I hear myself utter an order that I have already obeyed. For Levinas, all language points back to the opening to the Other figured in the formula, *here I am*. I bear witness [*le dire*] to saying in the simple "hello," or the "after you." Levinas hears a reference to the originary opening of ethics, of its very ethnicity, that must be contrasted with the order of discourse. The salutation of saying is not, he is adamant, "the stammering infancy of what will become the said, in which information will circulate" ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 199). It is not the inception of politics, of the political order, to which the said is linked, nor indeed the order, the subjectivity of the subject but their originary interruption.

[^32]: Thus, Levinas takes up another position with respect to the *polis*. He does not play dice with children in the temple of *phusis* or seek to rekindle the hearth, drawing the goddess Hestia out of the darkness and winning her protection. In saying, Levinas also discovers the relation to God; "to know God is to do justice to the neighbor" ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 199). For Levinas, the God of Judaic monotheism is revealed in the justice I render to the Other, in the pledge that always and already holds me hostage. God is there in the face of the Other, in the reading of scriptures and in the election that elects each of us to bear witness to his passing.

[^33]: This is how Levinas would lead us from the Heideggerian city, sobering us up, awakening us. But how does the call to attend to saying, to testifying to a prior bearing witness that occurs in the relation to the Other that is also a relation to God call us to transform our understanding of politics? One might assume that *Totality and Infinity* is simply a *refusal* of politics since the ethical, for Levinas, falls entirely outside the dimension of the political. However, in his "confessional" writings, Levinas risks venturing the possibility of a politics that would answer to monotheism, to the relation to the Other. He appeals to a monotheistic politics, to the convergence of the ethical and the political as the culmination of Zionism in the modern state of Israel.

[^34]: Levinas blames the decline of church-based religions on the advent of the political order, on the states of which the modern human being is a part. Zionism offers the possibility of bringing the political and the spiritual together. Thus, as he writes, "the Jewish people [...] achieves a State whose prestige none the less stems from the religion which modern political life supplants" ("Revelation in the Jewish Tradition" 261). This is why Levinas's Zionism is never merely nationalistic, philanthropic or humanitarian. Modern Israel provides more than a shelter for a people without a country: "beyond the sometimes astonishing, sometimes doubtful, accomplishments of the State of Israel, has it not been a matter above all of creating on its own land the concrete conditions for political innovation?" ("Difficult Freedom" 283). These sentences are as difficult to read now as at any time after 1947.[^4] But, for Levinas, this outward strength shelters the weaknesses of a Zionism uncompromised by *Realpolitik*. A star shines above Israel. The fate of this State, the mission of a monotheistic politics, is to climb like a living ladder towards that star. It is fragile because it is not closed on itself like a nation, because the interiority it possesses is nothing it possesses *for itself*.

[^35]: In this way, the star that crowns Levinasian Zionism is claimed to depend upon the destiny of the state of Israel, that is, upon what, first gathered the Jews and answered their mission. It depends upon the election of a particular race to the status of exemplar: to make them bear witness to monotheism as no other. But this is an election to a terrible vocation. The star is not the promise of a fulfillment of Sacred History in the sense that God would arrive in Israel like the Heideggerian god to its abode. This state would exhibit an exemplary patience maintaining the strange interiority of Israel in its openness, in the witnessing of the relation to the Other that is also the relation to God.

[^36]: The condition of ethical politics, of Levinasian monotheism, is an external force that maintains an exemplary balance with its interior, protecting it, permitting it to retain its purity, its relation to the exterior. The appeal to the outside does not, clearly enough, designate a space outside the state but marks what is outside the borders when its border is understood as a structural limit, as a marker of finitude, that would, also, for Levinas, attest to an opening to the Other and to God. God and the Other, otherwise than being, beyond essence, must remain unscathed, that is, pure and sacrosanct.

[^37]: But how can this be reconciled with patience, with the "eschatology of messianic peace" (*Totality and Infinity* 22) that Levinas so adamantly contrasts with "the peace of empires" in the preface to his first *magnum opus* (*Totality and Infinity* 22)?[^5]A messianic politics, the political innovation of the state of Israel, would answer to this eschatology. It is the return of a traumatic relation to the past that gives this eschatology its shape, determining its form and selecting its bearer.

[^38]: This model of the infinite as the incursion of a certain outside, bears a resemblance to the thought of the *polis* in Heidegger, in the sense that both point to an irruptive disclosure, to the upsurge of the god in the temple, of the flame in the hearth, to the infinite as infinite contestation. Levinas, of course, denies this. But there is, nevertheless, a *structural* similarity, an analogous configuration of the outside or repressed to which a particular people have a privileged relation in the work of the two philosophers. For both, in a parallel manner, the people in question would, with an absolute exemplarity, witness for all humankind. Likewise, both would attend to a saying that would demand protection *because of its vulnerability* -- demanding in turn an innovatory politics that would answer to an *ethos* upstream of classic determinations of ethics and politics. Both would point to a star as the "object" of a thought that opens a site that bears the possibility of a bestowal of a new ethics and a politics for a particular people.

My aim is show how this might disrupt any claim of *exemplarism* on the part of Heidegger and Levinas with respect to the Greeks and the Jews.

[^42]: Is philosophy really homesickness, the desire to be at home everywhere? In Heidegger and Levinas, this is still the case even if both emphasize a certain alterity. But Levinas, as I have shown, criticizes Heidegger's determination of the unhomely from the perspective of saying whilst producing a politics that bears a structural similarity to that of his adversary. There are, however, other conceptual resources in Levinas, linked, as I will show, to the work of his friend Blanchot, to whom he attributes a *critical* function with respect to Heidegger.

[^43]: In his early writings, Levinas writes of the impersonal *"il"* as the locus of an experience that suspends its mastery. The third person *"il"* persists as the "experiencer" of the *il y a*, absenting and effacing itself as locus. In this way, the "I" is disappropriated before it comes into existence; the outside, the *il y a* is always at play, continually threatening the disidentification of the "I." In a sense, the *"il"* names that which it is not in the power of the "I" to assume. The "I" is not a subject who stands counterposed to the world as an object. The *"il"* in the "I" is the originary site of an exposure, an opening to that which exceeds its measure. This refers simply to existence in general, to existence, as Levinas puts it, *without existents*, the *il y a*. The "I" opens from the *"il"* but does not leave it behind.[^6]

[^44]: In essays written in the same period as Levinas's early texts (which are themselves preceded by certain literary works to which Levinas refers[^7]), Blanchot proposes a account of the opening of language in terms of the *"il,"* the original donation, over which the "I" has no power. For Levinas, language is given as saying. But for Blanchot (perhaps a truer phenomenologist than Levinas) saying is indistinguishable from the *il y a*. The *il y a*, existence in general, affirms itself in this happening, placing it outside the determination of the "I." The disidentification with which Blanchot associates the experience of the *il y a* is figured in the refusal implicit in language to grant itself to the measure of the "I." In this sense, he does not merely activate a dormant notion in Levinas, using it to its own ends, but detaches it from the careful place to which the Jewish writer allots it, permitting it greater play and greater ambiguity. Commenting on Blanchot in turn, Levinas grants his work the status of a preliminary critique of Heideggerianism, that would clear the way for Levinasian philosophy. And indeed there is just such a critique, although it is implied, rather than being explicitly developed.

[^45]: For Heidegger, as I have indicated, *Dichtung* is not just poetry but a projective saying, a disposal or ordering of what is to be experiencable for a people. Basic words open up being as a whole, inventing a people in the openness they bestow.[^8] The creation of a people occurs in the opening of a language, in *Dichtung*; a people is alive for as long as those words resonate with their original, world-disclosive power. But whilst Heidegger thinks language in terms of the coming to presence or essencing of being and Levinas in terms of the encounter with the Other as saying, language is creative or projective for Blanchot in a different sense.

[^46]: Heidegger writes with great interpretative ingenuity on Hölderlin's hymnal poems, looking towards a new bestowal of a home for a German people. Hölderlin's place in Blanchot differs. In *The Space of Literature*, he is claimed to belong alongside Char and Rilke as a poet whose work figures the privilege of poetry with respect to nomination.[^9] Hölderlin invokes the elemental obscurity of language and, in so doing, the obscurity of being. Blanchot emphasizes the withdrawal that is bound up with this obscurity. The *il y a* names this withdrawal. It likewise names the welling up of the *"il"* in the "I." Language itself depends upon this originary *"il,"* on the knot tied between the "I" and the *il y a*. Now Blanchot follows Heidegger (although both ostensibly follow Hölderlin), in calling this withdrawal *earth*.[^10] But Blanchot differs from Heidegger in his determination of the *relation* between world and earth. For Heidegger, one might look towards a *Dichtung* that would constitute a genuine springing forth, an opening of the world for a people. For Blanchot, however, the poet cannot found a world, bringing it into a relation with earth and bestowing a new relation to the homely.[^11]

[^47]: Levinas is right to insist that the *il y a* cannot be reduced to the "abundance" of the Heideggerian *es gibt*; the *il y a* refers to "none of the generosity which the German term '*es gibt*' is said to contain" (*Ethics and Infinity* 47, "Difficult Freedom" 292). It is not linked to "the joy of what exists" (*Ethics and Infinity* 47) but to "the phenomenon of impersonal being," or what he calls in another essay, "horrible neutrality" ("Difficult Freedom" 292). But this does not mean that there is no correlate of the *il y a* in Heidegger's writings. The *il y a* is, as it were a *moment* of the self-disclosure of being. In the poem, for example, the *il y a* would name what *resists* appropriation -- the withdrawal that, for Heidegger, is always accompanied by an opening.

[^48]: Heidegger would have the Germans attend to a saying that would break through, that would attest to a co-belonging, to the intimacy of what gives and what refuses itself in this donation. This Heideggerian saying would pronounce a reserve "behind" ostensibly familiar words and hence the familiar projection of the world.  Is it to this saying that Blanchot points in writing of the *il y a*? Does he appeal to what, for Levinas, would be the generosity, abundance or joy of the *es gibt*?

[^49]: When Levinas emphatically separates the *il y a* from the Heideggerian *es gibt*, this can, as I have suggested, be read in terms of his separating out the elements of coming-to-presence in Heidegger. Blanchot's notion of the *il y a* belongs to the positioning of Levinas's ontology with respect to Heidegger's. As I have explained, the Heideggerian history of being opens and closes epochs by permitting a founding act to resonate *across an epoch*. An epoch lasts, indeed, only as long as this resonance occurs, that is, as long as a people are *attuned*.[^12] The history of being is a history of the opening and closing of such finite epochs.

[^50]: What form would a history of the *il y a* take? Since the *il y a* is sheer indetermination, a cratylean stream, then there is nothing from which history could be made. As the hither side, as the withdrawal of being, the *il y a* never enters into relation with its disclosure; it does not permit a founding act. In this sense it would be absurd to write a history of the *il y a*, since history is only possible if the withdrawal is thought *with* the disclosure, that is, if both are thought together in the relation Heidegger calls truth.

[^51]: It is significant that, when Levinas writes an essay in homage to Blanchot, he praises him for configuring the relationship between truth and errancy differently from Heidegger.[^13] It would seem that the *il y a*, naming what Heidegger calls earth, which is to say, one element in the happening of the truth of being, is emphasized over world in the work of Blanchot, challenging the possibility of the regulation of the relation between disclosure and withdrawal that belong to *Ereignis*.[^14] But this throws the *Seinsgeschichte* into disarray, since it becomes unclear whether there were founding moments of the kind upon which Heidegger insists. But it also prevents the new beginning for which Heidegger holds out. No Heideggerian God will come to save us. The *il y a* will return; but it does so in order to refuse to open itself into a hearth and indeed to open at all.

[^52]: It as if there were burning in the flames of the hearth a Revelation Heidegger would not welcome, an opening to the infinite that does not gather a people, but disperses it, expelling it outside the walls of the Heideggerian *polis*. But the notion of the *il y a*, insofar as it refers to the call from outside that threatens any settlement, any enrootedness or sedentarism, also turns upon the *Levinasian* city. Levinas would attend to the Biblical revelation, to the scriptures that are bound to the body of a people as strings to a violin. But Blanchot invites us to attend to a reserve of non-sense, to the outside, the *il y a*, as it is woven into the Biblical text and, indeed, into all texts and all expression in a way that is *upstream* of Levinasian saying.

[^53]: Levinas always associates the *il y a* with evil, repeating the classical horror of the infinite as the indeterminate and formless *aperion*. But the *il y a* is evil (in the sense, perhaps, that Bataille has allowed us to understand[^15]) only insofar as it resists, and, in so doing, prevents any attempt to hypostatize the movement of errancy or exile. Was this the call that called Abraham, gathering Israel together in the desert? Was it this call to which the Book attests and to which the people bound by the Book bear witness for the rest of humankind?

[^54]: It is a call, ultimately, that is linked with no particular people.[^16] Before the witnessing of the Book and preceding the sedentary civilizations from which the Jews were called, there was a demand without determinate shape or form. If it has a figure, it is that of an endless nomadic movement. In the end, however, it belongs to no one. It is simply an interruption of the sacred history of which the state of Israel would be the culmination. It takes no side, resisting the charge that Heidegger of the 1930s and the Levinas in his confessional writings alike accede to an exemplarism, that is, to the determination of a privileged bearer of alterity. The difficulty lies in the call for a particular land and a particular people to be the bearer of an unforeseen political innovation, for a chance that in turn demands the instigation and the protection of a vulnerable interior. But to raise walls against an exterior in order to protect an interior, to guard a *polis* in order to allow a people to attain its destiny, is to make a problematic demarcation. The problem lies in the very separation of the inner from the outer, that is, in the construction of walls that endangers the attendance to other locii, to spaces in transition, to other, unexpected foldings inside and outside the *polis*: to other groupings, unexpected and transitory communities, to chance mutations, proliferations and retractions of the social space. Are there other relations to the *il y a*, to the earth?

[^55]: Blanchot's practice of reading is a refusal of the exemplarism of both Heidegger and Levinas. Blanchot's poet-witness, the one whose writing must be witnessed in turn in its relation to the earth, to the *il y a*, is not the founder of a people who would permit in turn the setting up of the *polis*, the rekindling of a hearth, the inauguration of a temple. Blanchot's notion of reading invents a community of readers, not, he is adamant, a *Volk*, but a gathering of individuals each of whom is exposed to an irreducible openness. This non-people are not united by a shared memory, a shared civilization, or a shared myth. No cathedral, synagogue, no temple, no plastic form will contain them. They share no history, no solidarity; they belong neither to a religion nor to a nation. They are bound only by the reading that disperses them. They read, but what they read are signs and flashing indications. To what do they point? Not to the stars or to the astronomical order; but nor, simply, to the darkness that would exist before and after stars. They indicate the darkness that inhabits each star, to the earth from which the city is made that prevents either from settling into a pure relation.

[^56]: Levinas dreams of the star that would crown Israel; Heidegger of the star that would stand still in the world's sky. Above Blanchot's community[^17], no star can last more than an instant; the stars fall and their fiery trails illuminate the darkness from which they emerge and to which they return. The disaster obliterates stars even as they allow them to be born again; it makes of politics an attempt to attend to intermittencies not in order to invent new stars or extinguish them, but to draw these points of light back into their relation from the darkness from which they emerge and to which they will return. This disastrous politics has the empty form of an eschatology, of a patience that attests to the impossibility of situating any interiority with respect to the *il y a*.[^18] Is the Blanchovian community not the very opening of the outside understood as it exceeds the *polis* as city or state? Is it this *ethos* with which a *disastrous *politics would be linked?
